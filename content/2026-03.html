The Tennissance 🖊️
New York Review of Books—April 9, 2026
A review of Christopher Clarey's The Warrior: Rafael Nadal and His Kingdom of Clay and Giri Nathan's Changeover: A Young Rivalry and a New Era of Men's Tennis. Features some captivating behind-the-scenes episodes from the books, like Nadal entering the Roland Garros players' restaurant in 2021 "limping like a crippled guy" from chronic foot pain, and some geeky tennis trivia, like the fact that men born in the 1980s have won eighty Grand Slam titles, compared with only two for those born in the 1990s (I hadn't realized it was that lopsided). The piece also touches on tennis writing as a fading literary and journalistic practice, in part due to the social-media-enabled trend of players taking direct control of their own storytelling. Beautifully written itself, the piece is a reminder of how entertaining—and edifying—old-school sports journalism can be.
Stewart Brand's Whole Earth Catalog, the book that changed the world 🖊️
The Guardian—May 5, 2013
Though at times overly fawning, an entertaining look at the life and work of the American writer Stewart Brand, and the 1960s counterculture he came to epitomize. I especially appreciated the glimpses of the libertarian and individualistic ethos that people like Brand presaged; the phrase "Stay hungry, stay foolish", immortalized by Steve Jobs in his 2005 Stanford commencement address, was Brand's attempt to capture what he felt was the pinnacle of freedom: the lone hitchhiker on the endless open road, a bit hungry, a bit foolish.
What The AI Consciousness Question Conceals 🖊️
Noema—March 26, 2026
Contrary to the myth of automation, AI works best when combined with humans who exercise judgment. “The more urgent mythology is not the fantasy of conscious machines. It is the quiet, pervasive, economically devastating assumption that human presence in the loop is a cost rather than the source of compounding value.” An uplifting, grounded take.
What Are Psychedelic Entities? 🖊️
Noema—March 17, 2026
Foregrounded by the author's account of a psychedelic trip on the drug ibogaine, an exploration of what psychedelics reveal about the history and limits of the modern scientific worldview and the workings of the human brain.
Bottling the World Economy 🖊️
New York Review of Books—March 23, 2026
Against the backdrop of the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, a look at how Gulf countries have become critical to the global economy beyond their traditional role as suppliers of oil and natural gas.
The Blizzard 📕
Vladimir Sorokin
A pandemic has broken out in the remote Russian town of Dolgoye, and Doctor Platon Ilich is on a mission to deliver the vaccine that will save it. Amid a raging blizzard, a rustic farmhand named Crouper offers to take him there—on a sled drawn by fifty miniature horses. But their journey is soon derailed, in no small part due to the seemingly harmless indulgences the doctor partakes in along the way: a sexual affair here, a narcotics trip there. A dark allegory of the human condition.
Tomorrow is Yesterday: Life, Death, and the Pursuit of Peace in Israel/Palestine 📕
Hussein Agha and Robert Malley
A retrospective on the Oslo peace process and the two-state paradigm it envisioned in Israel-Palestine. All but officially defunct and discredited, the authors suggest that it was ultimately inherently flawed from the very beginning.
