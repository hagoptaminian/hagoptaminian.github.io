Why AI Needs A Sense Of Smell 🖊️
Noema—April 16, 2026
On the wonders and mysteries of our sense of smell. Dismissed by Darwin as being "of extremely slight service" to us, it emerges here as a sense rich in hidden power. Humans can detect a single odor molecule at a concentration of 0.01 parts per billion—about one in 100 trillion air molecules. Smell is essential to tasting food; it helps us choose romantic partners, and evidence shows that certain diseases, like Parkinson's, can be detected by odor alone. The piece also explores the work of researchers and startups seeking to capture and record smell, perhaps to one day fold it into AIs trained on richer 'world models'.

The Damascus Events: The 1860 Massacre and the Destruction of the Old Ottoman World 📕
Eugene Rogan
A highly readable account of the transformations sweeping through Syria, then a province of the Ottoman Empire, in the nineteenth century. A growing European commercial presence in Damascus undercuts the city’s artisan class and increasingly favors its Christian community as diplomatic representatives and intermediaries. Meanwhile, driven in part by fears of European intervention on behalf of the Empire’s Christians, the Ottoman government enacts the Tanzimat reforms, granting legal equality to its citizens regardless of religion—thus reversing centuries of precedent and, in the eyes of many Muslims, contravening Islamic law. Tensions boil over in the summer of 1860, when Muslim mobs massacre thousands of Damascene Christians and plunder and burn their homes: the book’s eponymous “events.” But more than just an account of those events, Rogan’s book is a history of the Levant in the late Ottoman period, as European power, Ottoman reform, and new ideas of political equality increasingly unsettle the old imperial order—while also generating a period of urban, institutional, and civic renewal in response.
