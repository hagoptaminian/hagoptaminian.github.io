The War That Made the Middle East 📕
Mustafa Aksakal
An account of the years of World War I in the Ottoman Empire that is at once gripping narrative and incisive history. Though not neglecting major events like the military campaigns at Sarıkamış, Suez, and Gallipoli, the book stands out for also capturing the smaller details of wartime life across the Empire: the meetings of the Ottoman parliament, the effects of famine and conscription on the Empire's citizens, the War's impact on imperial finances, and the role of German commanders and officers in the Ottoman theater.
Cities of Salt 📕
Abdelrahman Munif
The epic tale of how two towns in the Arabian Desert—in an unnamed Arab kingdom—are transformed by the discovery of oil in the 1930s. In the harsh, seemingly endless desert, Wadi Al-Oyoun is an ancient oasis village that has long offered refuge to travelers, pilgrims, and trade caravans. Nearby Harran is a desolate coastal outpost with blistering summers. Their people live austere lives ordered by custom, faith, and the rhythms of the desert. Then American oil prospectors arrive. Published in Arabic in 1984, the novel’s dramatization of that encounter offers an illuminating account of the region's first collision with oil capitalism, at a time when older social and moral worlds were still intact enough to register the shock.
