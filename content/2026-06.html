The Mars Delusion 🖊️
Noema—June 18, 2026
On the perennial dream of settling Mars. It’s a tempting prospect: to pause and contemplate the existence of the planets, out there in space, and the possibility that we could go there—and who knows where after, and what mysteries and secrets we will uncover? Mars appears especially inviting: photos it suggest a familiar desert landscape. But… as this piece shows, Mars is profoundly unfit for human life: cold and distant, its air laden with carbon dioxide, its atmosphere so thin that the water in our bodies would evaporate on contact with the planet. It’s a chilling, suffocating prospect, and one which makes you appreciate life on this planet—how special and unbelievably strange and unlikely it is to be here—a little more.

The forging of countries 🖊️
Aeon—September 20, 2024
Why one prominent 20th-century historian of nationalism, Hans Kohn, was mistaken when he theorized that nationalism in Western states like Britain, France, and the U.S. was fundamentally civic and inclusive, while nationalism in non-Western states in Central and Eastern Europe was ethnic and exclusive. The piece argues that this split is too neat: many Central and Eastern European polities in the 19th and 20th centuries attempted civic forms of nationalism, but were often constrained by messy demographics and geography, while Kohn's Western paragons were themselves often based on the dominance of one ethnic or linguistic group.
Therapy for billionaires 🖊️
Aeon—June 15, 2026
A ranging and thought-provoking reflection on wealth, privilege, inheritance, and class. It's interesting how these often reveal themselves in subtle or unspoken details and cues: the size of one's house, the way one peels a pear (the writer's father, from a humble background: meticulous and frugal; her mother, from a privileged one: hurried and unfussed), and the little background assumptions about how the world works and what's possible and permissible for a person. For many people in this piece, wealth and inheritance are a gilded cage—and their struggle to escape it raises compelling questions about money, its purpose, and what freedom actually means.

Atatürk 📕
Andrew Mango
Born in polyglot, multiethnic Ottoman Salonica in 1880-81, by the time Mustafa Kemal dies in 1938 the Ottoman world he was born into is a distant memory. Salonica is long gone, lost in 1912 to Greece; its Muslims, along with those from the rest of Greece, are soon sent to the new Republic of Turkey—which sends 1.5 million Greeks the other way. The Ottoman Sultanate is no more; the old Empire’s Arab provinces are divided by colonial powers into dubious, tentative new states. Anatolia’s Armenian community is uprooted and scattered. Coinciding with and often shaping these events, Atatürk’s life provides a compelling glimpse into the transition from empire to nation in the Near East and old Ottoman world.
